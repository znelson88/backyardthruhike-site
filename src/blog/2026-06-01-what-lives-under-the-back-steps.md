The gap between the bottom step and the soil is about two inches. I'd walked past it ten thousand times without stopping.

When I finally did stop — crouched down with a flashlight at dusk — I counted seven pill bugs, a wolf spider the size of a quarter, one small millipede working its way along the edge of the concrete, and something I couldn't identify moving fast enough that I lost it.

None of this is remarkable in a scientific sense. These are common animals. But what struck me was how much activity was happening in a space I'd never once thought to look at. Two inches of gap. A complete small world.

This is the basic idea behind everything on this site: that proximity is not the same as familiarity. You can live next to something for years and know almost nothing about it. The back steps are a good place to start.

**What to look for:**

- Pill bugs (also called roly-polies or woodlice) are crustaceans, not insects — more closely related to crabs than to beetles. They breathe through gills and need moisture, which is why they're under things, not on top of them.
- Wolf spiders don't build webs. They hunt. The two large eyes at the front of their face are diagnostic — most spiders have smaller, more uniform eyes.
- Millipedes move slowly and eat decaying plant matter. They're harmless and actually useful. Centipedes move fast and can bite — they're predators.

Spend five minutes. You'll see something.

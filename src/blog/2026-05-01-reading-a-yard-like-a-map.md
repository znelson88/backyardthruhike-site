---
title: Reading a yard like a map
date: 2026-05-01
type: Field note
summary: How to find the edges, the corridors, the places where two kinds of habitat meet. These are the spots worth paying attention to.
---

Ecologists talk about "edges" — the places where two habitat types meet. A forest edge where it meets a meadow. A pond margin where water meets land. These transition zones tend to have more species than either habitat on its own, because they offer resources from both.

Your yard has edges too, even if they're small.

The line where mowed grass meets an unmowed patch. The gap between the fence and the soil. The base of a tree where bark gives way to ground. The crack in the driveway where a weed has pushed through. These are your edges, and they're worth mapping.

**A simple exercise:**

Walk your yard slowly and note where one surface type becomes another. You're not looking for anything in particular yet — just building a map in your head of where the transitions are. Mark them if you want. Edges: fence line, base of the oak, the wet corner where the downspout drains, the strip between the sidewalk and the street.

Then come back to those spots over a few weeks. Note what you find there versus what you find in the middle of the lawn. The difference will be significant.

**Corridors** are the other thing to look for — narrow strips of cover that connect two larger areas. A row of shrubs along a fence. The strip of weeds along the back wall. Animals use these to move between spaces without crossing open ground. If you want to support wildlife in a small space, corridors matter more than area.

A yard is not a random collection of plants and soil. It has structure. Learning to read that structure changes what you see.

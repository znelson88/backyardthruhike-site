---
title: Why distance isn't the point
date: 2026-04-01
type: Essay
summary: National parks matter. But the idea that nature only counts when it's far away and spectacular has done a lot of quiet damage.
---

There's a version of nature writing that requires a permit.

You drive to the trailhead, you check in at the ranger station, you hike to the designated viewpoint, you take the photograph. The experience is real. The place is genuinely extraordinary. And it's completely inaccessible to most people most of the time.

This isn't an argument against national parks. They matter enormously — as refuges for species, as repositories of landscape, as places where some of what the continent used to look like has been allowed to persist. The argument for protecting them is strong and I believe it.

But there's a side effect that doesn't get discussed much: when nature is framed primarily as something far away and spectacular, it becomes easy to conclude that the nature near you doesn't count. The vacant lot doesn't count. The storm drain creek doesn't count. The weed growing through the sidewalk doesn't count. Only the parks count.

This has consequences. If the nature in your immediate surroundings feels like a lesser version of the real thing, you're less likely to pay attention to it, less likely to protect it, and less likely to notice when it disappears.

The birds that use your neighborhood are real birds. The insects in your garden are real insects. The soil under your lawn is alive — genuinely, measurably alive, with more organisms in a teaspoon than there are people on earth.

None of this is a substitute for wild places. But the idea that proximity makes something less worth noticing is one worth examining.

This site is an attempt to notice what's close.

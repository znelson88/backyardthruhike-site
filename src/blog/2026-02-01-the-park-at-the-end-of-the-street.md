---
title: The park at the end of the street
date: 2026-02-01
type: Essay
summary: A small urban green space that most people walk past without stopping. What you find when you do stop, and why it matters that it's there.
---

At the end of the street where I grew up, there was a small park. Not a destination park — no playground, no sports field, no feature that would appear on a map of the city. Just a rectangle of grass with a few trees at the edges and a path worn across the middle by people taking the shorter route to the bus stop.

Most people who used that path never stopped. I didn't, for years.

When I did start stopping, I found: a red-tailed hawk that used one of the oaks as a regular perch. A colony of ants in the southeast corner that had been there, I later found out, long enough that people in the neighborhood remembered them from childhood. In spring, a patch of wild violets along the north edge that bloomed for about three weeks and then disappeared until the following year.

None of these things required anything of me except time and the decision to stand still.

Small urban parks are often treated as maintenance problems — spaces that have to be mowed and cleaned and kept safe. The ecological function they serve gets much less attention. But even a modest rectangle of grass with a few trees provides: shelter for migrating birds moving through in spring and fall, a break in the impervious surface that lets rain soak in rather than run off, a small population of insects that in turn support other animals.

The park at the end of your street is doing work that isn't visible in the mowing schedule.

It's worth knowing what's there.

---
title: Starting a nature log
date: 2026-03-01
type: How-to
summary: You don't need special equipment. A notebook, consistent timing, and a willingness to sit still are most of what it takes.
---

The most useful thing you can do when you start paying attention to local nature is to write things down.

Not because the records will be scientifically valuable (though they might be, over time). But because the act of writing makes you look more carefully. When you know you're going to describe something, you look at it longer.

**What you need:**

A notebook. Any notebook — a composition book from a drugstore works fine. A pen. That's it. The rest is optional.

**What to write:**

Date, time, weather, location. Then what you saw. Then, separately, what you noticed — which is different from what you saw. "A bird" is what you saw. "It landed on the fence, sat for about thirty seconds, then flew toward the oak tree" is what you noticed.

Don't worry about identifying everything. Note the shape, the size, the color, the behavior. You can look it up later, or you might never identify it, and that's fine. The observation itself is the point.

**Consistency matters more than frequency:**

Going out for ten minutes every morning at the same time will teach you more than occasional longer trips. Patterns emerge over time — which birds are present in which months, when the first particular insect appears in spring, how the character of the yard changes between wet and dry periods. You can't see patterns without repeated observations.

**A simple format to start:**

```
Date: March 4
Time: 7:15am
Weather: overcast, 42°F, no wind
Location: back yard, near the fence

Saw: two dark-eyed juncos on the ground under the feeder. 
One seemed larger. They moved apart when I came outside, 
then came back within two minutes.
```

That's enough. Do that for a month and see what accumulates.
